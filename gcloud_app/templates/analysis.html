Analysis & Key Findings

Importance of Finding Relevant Demographics

Frequent flyers are more invested in their airline experience. Although business travel only accounts for approximately 30% of US air travel, 70% of the responses were from business travelers. When traveling in business class, business travelers on average are more satisfied than people traveling for leisure. However, the opposite is true when traveling in economy class. It is understandable that people flying in business class with their own money are pickier about the experience provided, thus personal business class travel metrics might be more relevant in finding pain points and in how to further improve the business class customer experience. The demographics with the biggest deviation from overall correlation coefficients are:

  • Non-frequent business class passengers traveling for leisure (critical)
  • Non-frequent economy class passengers traveling for business (critical)
  • Frequent business class passengers traveling for business (favorable)

    • Different Features Matter to Different People

    Correlating the number of air passengers to a specific survey sample might give clues as to which features need to be improved to satisfy the critical masse. For example, frequent flyers traveling in economy class for leisure make up 29% of survey respondents, yet they are 15% and 10% less satisfied with online check-in and booking than the average passenger, respectively. However, their satisfaction with inflight wifi service is 20% greater than the average which indicates these passengers are very online literate and spend a lot of time online. In other words services that have been moved from in-person to online have not produced the desired result with the very audience they were intended for. The individual features where satisfaction or dissatisfaction most deviates from the average are:

  • Satisfaction with flight schedule for non-frequent business class passengers traveling for leisure: potential to charge more
  • Dissatisfaction with flight schedule for non-frequent economy class passengers traveling for leisure: they had to buy the cheapest flight per company rules
  • Satisfaction with seat comfort for non-frequent business class passengers traveling for business: happy to be out of economy class
  • Dissatisfaction with seat comfort for non-frequent business class passengers traveling for leisure: seat doesn't feel worth the extra money paid

    • What Affects Overall Satisfaction or Dissatisfaction

    In addition to looking at individual features or their correlation and importance to different demographics, it is also possible to investigate the effect of features or flight delays on overall customer satisfaction. This makes it easier to visualize and understand which features are must-win battles in gaining overall customer satisfaction and which ones are "nice to have" features. For e.g.

  • Even if satisfied with original flight schedule, baggage handling and food/drinks, it is possible that the customer is overall dissatisfied
  • Even when dissatisfied with gate location it is still possible to be satisfied overall
  • Being dissatisfied with features such as online check-in, leg room and seat comfort seem to be deal breakers
